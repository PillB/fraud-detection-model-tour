THE CHALLENGE

Why standard ML fails at fraud

Fraud incidence in financial transactions typically ranges from 0.01% to 2%. A trivial “all legitimate” classifier attains >99% accuracy while detecting zero fraud events.

Production-grade systems must detect known patterns (velocity bursts, mule accounts) alongside novel attacks (zero-day schemes), operate under extreme class imbalance, accommodate concept drift, and produce auditable, explainable scores for regulatory scrutiny.

METRIC OF CHOICE
PR-AUC
Focuses on the rare positive class. ROC-AUC is misleading on imbalanced data.

NEW TO FRAUD DETECTION ML?

The 90-second plain English version

Imagine Alice buys a $5 coffee every weekday at 8am using her usual card and phone. One night at 3:17am a $4,800 charge appears at a new merchant in another country from a device she has never used, right after a new linked account was added.

Most transactions are normal. Fraud is rare (often <1-2%). A model that always says “legit” would be 98-99% accurate yet useless. The goal is to rank the truly suspicious cases high enough that investigators can review the top 50–200 alerts and catch the real fraud without drowning in false alarms.

This site walks through the main techniques banks and fintechs actually use — from simple velocity rules and trees to representation learning, graphs for collusion rings, and hybrids — with real runnable code and the metrics that matter on imbalanced data (PR-AUC first).

Key terms you will see: PR-AUC (how well the model ranks the rare bad cases — random guessing scores roughly the fraud rate), velocity (how fast activity is happening), KYA/KYE (know-your-account / know-your-entity signals about the people and businesses involved).

XGBoost
SUPERVISED BASELINE
Tier 1

Gradient-boosted trees constitute the production standard for labeled tabular fraud. Strong feature engineering—velocity, amount deviation, KYA aggregates—yields high PR-AUC on known patterns while delivering native feature importances and SHAP explanations.

GENERATIVE ANOMALY
Tier 3

VAE trained exclusively on legitimate transactions learns a probabilistic manifold of normal behavior. Latent representations also serve as high-value features for downstream supervised models.

BEHAVIORAL
Tier 5

Recurrent models capture temporal dependencies across a user’s transaction history. Sudden deviations in velocity, amount patterns, or merchant sequences become detectable even when individual transactions appear benign. Essential for modeling drift and behavioral fraud.

TabTransformer
TABULAR DL
Tier 4

Self-attention applied to categorical features (merchant type, KYA risk buckets, time-of-day) produces contextual embeddings. Captures complex interactions that tree models require explicit cross-features to detect. Highly effective when tabular data includes rich categorical signals from KYA/KYE profiles.

Representative PR-AUC: 0.278
Educational simulation using embeddings + classifier; attention provides feature-level interpretability.

GRAPH / RELATIONAL
Tier 4

Inductive graph neural network that learns node embeddings via neighbor sampling and aggregation. Critical for detecting collusion rings, mule networks, and shared-device or KYA-linked fraud that is invisible to purely tabular models. Naturally handles new entities at inference time.

Metrics that matter

PR-AUC + operational context

PR-AUC is primary because it focuses on the rare positive class. On 1.2% fraud data, random guessing scores 0.012. A model with 0.28–0.37 is meaningfully better at surfacing fraud in the top of a review queue.

Production framing: Review budget is limited (e.g. top-K=50–200 alerts). Recall@K tells you what fraction of actual fraud you catch within budget. Cost framing (Bahnsen-style): FN ≈ fraud amount lost; FP ≈ review/investigator cost + customer friction. Hybrids and good FE improve both lift and queue efficiency.

Always report vs. random baseline and vs. simple rules. Use temporal splits for evaluation. See junior_starter.py and compare_models.py for runnable scripts with these metrics.

Key Terms Glossary
PR-AUC: Precision-Recall Area Under Curve. Primary metric for imbalanced fraud detection (focuses on catching the rare frauds). Random baseline ≈ fraud rate (0.012 here).
Velocity: Rate of activity (e.g. tx counts in last 1h/24h). Sudden spikes are strong fraud signals.
KYA / KYE: Know Your Account / Know Your Entity. Relational signals about users, merchants, workers, links — used for collusion detection.
Recall@K: Fraction of all fraud caught when reviewing only the top K highest-risk cases. Critical for limited review budgets.
Layered Cascades: Fast cheap gate (rules/IF) first, then heavier models only on uncertain cases. Production standard for latency + coverage.
Hybrid / MoE: Combining multiple model families (e.g. trees + generative + graph) with routing or fusion for better coverage across fraud types.

1. Load & Clean
Generate synthetic transactional + entity + graph data. Clean missing values. Profile distributions and injected fraud archetypes (velocity, amount deviation, relational). Reproducible with fixed seeds.
2. Feature Engineering
Core signals: velocity (1h/24h counts), amount statistics vs. history, temporal (hour, night), KYA risk proxies. StandardScaler + OneHot. Feature engineering routinely outperforms model swaps (Bahnsen et al.).
3. Modeling
Parallel training across families: supervised (XGBoost), tabular proxies, Isolation Forest (anomaly gate), ensemble meta-learner. Covers known patterns, novel deviations, and relational signals.
4. Ensemble + Output
Late score fusion (supervised + anomaly). Report PR-AUC for ensemble and hybrid. Rank highest-risk transactions with multi-view explanations (importance, reconstruction, attention). Foundation for production cascades.

Core Insights from Research & Experiments
  • • Feature engineering (velocity + entity aggregates) often yields larger gains than switching model families.
  • • Hybrids dominate: generative models surface novel fraud; supervised models surface known patterns.
  • • Graph layers are essential once KYA/KYE data exists; they close coverage gaps tabular models cannot see.
  • • PR-AUC and Recall@K matter far more than accuracy or even ROC-AUC in production settings.
When to Choose What
Start here: XGBoost + IF on engineered features.
Need novel detection: VAE or Isolation Forest hybrids.
Rich categorical / KYA: TabTransformer embeddings.
Behavioral / temporal: LSTM-style or sequence models.
Collusion / rings: GraphSAGE or TGN family.
Complex mixed fraud: MoE or cascaded ensembles.
